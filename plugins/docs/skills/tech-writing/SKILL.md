# Technical Writing

Universal rules for technical prose: documentation, design documents, PRs, wiki pages, and work items. Grounded in the [Google Technical Writing courses](https://developers.google.com/tech-writing).

For project-specific voice and team calibration, pair this skill with a `writing-style` skill in your own project's `.claude/skills/` (fork-and-customize pattern).

## When to use

- Drafting any technical prose — documentation, design docs, PR descriptions, wiki pages, or work items.
- Reviewing prose for clarity, active voice, and structure before publishing.
- Tightening an existing document: removing filler, splitting overlong sentences, fixing heading order.

---

## Words

### Choose terms carefully

Define any term the document introduces that readers may not know. If introducing three or more project-specific terms, include a glossary near the opening.

Use the same word for the same concept throughout. Renaming mid-document creates the same confusion as an inconsistent variable name in code.

### Acronyms

Define an acronym on first use only if: (a) it is meaningfully shorter than the full term, and (b) it appears many times in the document. First-use format: bold both the expanded form and the acronym in parentheses — **Protocol Buffers (PB)**. After introduction, use the acronym consistently; don't revert to the full term.

Don't define acronyms used only a few times. Use the full term instead.

### Active voice

Prefer active voice: actor + verb + target. Passive voice buries the actor and lengthens sentences.

| Passive (avoid) | Active (prefer) |
|---|---|
| "Read-only access is provided by MutableInput" | "MutableInput provides read-only access" |
| "A wrapper is generated by the Op registration process" | "The Op registration process generates a wrapper" |
| "Code is interpreted by Python, but code is compiled by C++" | "Python interprets code, but C++ compiles code" |

Imperative verbs are active: "Open the config file" has an implied actor (the reader) and is always active voice.

### Strong verbs

The verb is the most important part of a sentence. Use specific verbs. Replace weak forms — especially "be," "occur," "happen," and "There is / There are" — with precise alternatives.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "An error **occurs** when dividing by zero." | "Dividing by zero **raises** the exception." |
| "There is a variable called `met_trick` that stores the accuracy." | "The `met_trick` variable stores the current accuracy." |
| "Setting this flag makes the application run **fast**." | "Setting this flag makes the application run 225% faster." |

Replace subjective adjectives and adverbs with objective data. "Very fast" tells no one anything; "225% faster" does.

## Sentences

### One idea per sentence

Each sentence should convey one primary thought. When a sentence contains two independent ideas joined by a conjunction, split it.

### Remove filler

Cut phrases that occupy space without adding information:

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| at this point in time | now |
| is able to | can |
| in spite of the fact that | although |
| provides a detailed description of | describes |
| causes the triggering of | triggers |

### Subordinate clauses

Ask: does the subordinate clause extend the main idea, or branch to a separate one? If it branches, split it.

- Extends (keep): "Python is interpreted, which means it executes line by line."
- Branches (split): "Bash is a modern shell, which was developed at Bell Labs in the 1970s." → "Bash is a modern shell. Bell Labs developed it in the 1970s."

### "That" vs. "which" (US English)

- **That** introduces essential (restrictive) clauses — no comma, no pause.
- **Which** introduces nonessential (nonrestrictive) clauses — comma before, pause when read aloud.

"The flag **that** controls logging is named `verbose`." (the clause identifies which flag)

"The `verbose` flag, **which** controls logging, defaults to false." (the flag is already identified; the clause adds a parenthetical fact)

Test: read aloud. If you'd naturally pause before the clause, use "which" and add a comma.

### Pronoun distance

Place pronouns as close as possible to their antecedents. If more than five words separate a pronoun from its antecedent, restate the noun instead.

Watch "it," "they," "them," "their," "this," and "that" — these are the most common culprits.

- Bad: "Python is interpreted, while C++ is compiled. **It** has an almost cult-like following." ("It" could refer to Python or C++)
- Fix: "Python has an almost cult-like following."

When "this" or "that" stands alone, follow it with a noun: "**This user ID** lets users authenticate."

## Paragraphs

The opening sentence is the most important in the paragraph. Readers scan first sentences and skip the rest. If the opening sentence doesn't establish the point, the paragraph is effectively invisible.

Target 3–5 sentences per paragraph. Avoid walls of text (7+ sentences) and avoid isolated one-liners where context is needed.

Test each paragraph against three questions:

- **What** is the core message?
- **Why** does the reader need this information?
- **How** should the reader apply or verify it?

A paragraph that can't answer all three is either incomplete or doing two things at once. Split it, or supply the missing element.

## Audience

Model your reader before you draft.

**The formula:** Good documentation = what the audience needs to know − what they already know.

Before writing, identify:
- Who is the reader (role, specialization)?
- What task or question brings them to this document?
- What do they already know about this topic?
- What will they know or be able to do after reading?

The gap between need and existing knowledge is what you write. Don't explain what they already know; don't skip what they'll need.

**Curse of knowledge:** Experts unconsciously assume that readers share years of accumulated context. If a reader doesn't, they miss the subtext you didn't know you were including. Spell out what's implied — unless you've confirmed the reader already has it.

**Idioms and cultural references:** Avoid sports metaphors, regional expressions, and archaic phrases. They fail for non-native readers and international teams.

## Scope

State what the document covers at the opening. Then explicitly state what it does NOT cover.

Both parts matter. The scope statement tells the reader what to expect. The non-scope statement tells them not to look here for content that lives somewhere else, which prevents wasted reading time and misplaced frustration.

Exclude only topics readers would realistically expect to find. An unreasonable non-scope statement ("this document does not cover the history of TCP/IP") adds noise without helping anyone.

## Organization

**Outline before you draft.** Organizing after writing is harder and produces weaker structure. Outline at the topic level, share with reviewers, then draft.

Structure content in reader relevance order — not in the order you learned it, discovered it, or wrote it.

**Introduction requirements:** Every document's opening should cover:
1. What this document covers.
2. What the reader needs to know coming in (prerequisites).
3. What this document does not cover.

**Headings:** Use task-based language describing what the reader does: "Configure the build environment," not "Build environment configuration." Use noun phrases for reference docs. Add a brief sentence of context under each heading before jumping into sub-headings or lists — readers need a foothold before details.

**Progressive disclosure:** Move from simple to complex. Introduce new terminology near where it's first needed. Break dense sections with lists, tables, and diagrams.

## Lists and Tables

**Bulleted lists:** For unordered items where rearranging doesn't change meaning.

**Numbered lists:** For ordered items where sequence matters. Start each item with an imperative verb.

**Parallelism:** Every item in a list must match in grammatical form, logical category, capitalization, and end-punctuation.

- Good: Carrots / Potatoes / Cabbages
- Bad: Carrots / Potatoes / The summer light obscures all memories of winter.

**Avoid embedded lists.** A run-in series ("you can configure X, Y, or Z") should almost always become a bulleted list.

**Introduce every list and table** before it appears. End the introductory sentence with a colon. Use "the following" when natural: "The following table compares the two options."

**Tables:** Use for three or more structured data points per item — content the reader needs to look up or compare. Keep cells concise (two sentences max). Use meaningful column headers. Don't use a table to restate what the preceding prose already said.

## Punctuation (US English)

**Commas:** Insert where you'd naturally pause. Use the Oxford (serial) comma before the last item in a series. Never join two independent clauses with a comma alone — that's a comma splice. Use a period or semicolon instead.

**Semicolons:** Connect two closely related independent clauses. Both sides must stand alone as complete sentences. If one side is incomplete, use a comma. When a transition word follows a semicolon, add a comma after it: "Rerun Frambus after updating config; however, don't rerun after updating source code."

**Em dashes:** Signal a longer pause than a comma. Use paired em dashes to set off a digression: "C++ is a rich language — one requiring extensive experience to master." Don't stack them; they interrupt flow when overused.

**Colons:** Introduce lists and tables. The sentence before the colon must be grammatically complete on its own.

**Parentheses:** Hold minor digressions only. If the content matters, move it to the main text. Period placement: inside the parenthesis if the entire sentence is enclosed; outside if the parenthesis ends a larger sentence.

**Hyphens:** Connect words in compound modifiers (decision-making system, self-attention). Check a dictionary for ambiguous cases; three-word compounds often hyphenate only the first two words.

## Self-Editing

Drafting and editing are different cognitive tasks — separate the passes.

**Revision order:**
1. **Structure:** Is the information in the right order? Are the right things omitted?
2. **Clarity:** Active voice? Specific verbs? One idea per sentence?
3. **Tightening:** Remove filler. Shorten sentences. Extract embedded lists.
4. **Read-aloud:** Awkward phrasing surfaces when spoken. This is the final polish pass.

**Perspective reset:** Set the draft aside before the final edit. Change how you read it — different font, printed page, different device. Fresh eyes catch errors that stale ones don't.

**Peer review:** A reviewer doesn't need to be a subject-matter expert to improve prose. They need only to flag where they got confused.

## Self-Check

Before delivering any technical prose:

1. Is every new term defined at first use? Are project-specific acronyms bolded in both expanded and short form on first mention?
2. Is every sentence active voice, unless passive is the deliberate choice?
3. Do verbs carry the action, or are you leaning on "is," "occurs," or "there is"?
4. Does every sentence carry one idea? Do subordinate clauses extend the main point, or branch away from it?
5. Do "that" and "which" follow the US English comma rule?
6. Is every pronoun within five words of its antecedent? Does "this" or "that" stand alone without a following noun?
7. Does each paragraph open with a sentence that states its point? Does the paragraph answer what / why / how?
8. Have you modeled the reader — who they are, what they need, what they already know?
9. Does the opening declare what the document covers AND what it doesn't?
10. Is the document structured for the reader, not in the order you learned or discovered the content?
